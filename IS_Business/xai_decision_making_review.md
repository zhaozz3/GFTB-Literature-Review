## IS / Business Literature Example: Explainable AI for Enhanced Decision-Making

**Bibliographic Information**

- **Title**: Explainable AI for enhanced decision-making
- **Authors**: Kristof Coussement, Mohammad Zoynul Abedin, Mathias Kraus, Sebastian Maldonado, Kazim Topuz
- **Year**: 2024
- **Venue**: *Decision Support Systems*, 184, 114276
- **DOI**: 10.1016/j.dss.2024.114276
- **Research Topic**: explainable AI; decision support; business decision-making; IS research; XAI dimensions; future research agenda
- **Recommended APA Citation**: Coussement, K., Abedin, M. Z., Kraus, M., Maldonado, S., & Topuz, K. (2024). Explainable AI for enhanced decision-making. *Decision Support Systems, 184*, 114276. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.dss.2024.114276
- **Note**: This paper does not propose a new algorithm. Instead, it is an editorial / positioning paper for a DSS special issue, and its value lies in helping researchers situate XAI within business decision-making and IS / decision-support research.

The core purpose of this paper is not to introduce a new explainable AI method, but to address a higher-level question: why explainable AI (XAI) matters for business decision-making and information systems research. Coussement et al. (2024) define XAI as a process that enables people to understand how AI systems decide, predict, and operate. They argue that as organizations increasingly rely on AI for operational and strategic decisions, explainability is no longer just a technical add-on; it becomes a condition for adoption, trust, governance, and decision quality. The paper therefore performs an important framing function: it shifts XAI from being treated as a narrow algorithmic interpretability topic toward being understood as a core issue in decision support and managerial relevance.

From an innovation perspective, the contribution of the paper lies not in proposing a new model, but in building a discussion framework that IS and business researchers can use. The authors organize the XAI-for-decision-making space into three dimensions:

- **Data**
- **Method**
- **Application**

The data dimension concerns the sources, quality, biases, and representations of the information behind explanations. The method dimension concerns how explanations are generated, displayed, and interacted with. The application dimension concerns how explanations influence real business decision processes and outcomes in context. The main value of the paper is thus that it provides a way to translate XAI research from "model explanation techniques" into "decision-support artifact and business value" research.

## Research Logic Chain

- **Problem gap**: XAI research is growing rapidly, but much of it remains focused on technical explanation methods without strong connection to business decision-making and IS artifact value.
- **Why prior work was insufficient**: Focusing only on transparency or explanation algorithms does not fully answer why organizations need XAI, how XAI improves decision-making, or how its managerial value should be evaluated.
- **Core idea of the paper**: Place XAI within the decision-support context and organize future research through three dimensions: data, method, and application.
- **Implementation chain**:
  1. Define the role of AI and XAI in business decision-making;
  2. Explain that XAI is not just about interpreting models, but about enabling better decisions;
  3. Propose the three-dimensional framing of data, method, and application;
  4. Use the papers in the special issue to illustrate these dimensions;
  5. Derive a future research agenda for IS scholars.
- **Validation logic**: As an editorial, the paper does not validate a method through experiments. Instead, it builds its argument through conceptual integration, structured categorization of special-issue contributions, and the construction of a future research agenda.

## Key Terms

- **XAI (Explainable AI)**: the process that enables humans to understand how an AI system predicts, decides, and performs its operations.
- **Decision support**: the use of information systems to help individuals or organizations make better decisions.
- **Business decision-making**: the operational, tactical, and strategic decisions made in organizations; this is the paper's core business context.
- **Data dimension**: issues related to the source, quality, bias, and representation of data used in or behind explanations.
- **Method dimension**: how explanations are generated, displayed, and interacted with.
- **Application dimension**: how XAI is implemented in concrete business settings and how it affects decision processes and outcomes.
- **Managerial relevance**: the extent to which research creates real value for management practice and business decision-making.

## Data / Research Setting

This paper is not a standard empirical study, so it does not involve a training dataset or benchmark dataset in the conventional sense. Its "research setting" is better understood as:

- **a framing / editorial setting for a Decision Support Systems special issue**
- **a synthesis of XAI research across multiple business decision-making domains**

The paper refers to application contexts such as:

- medical prediction
- remaining useful life prediction
- student dropout prediction
- human trafficking identification
- anti-money-laundering detection

These examples do not form a unified dataset; rather, they serve to show that XAI is already entering multiple high-impact decision domains. In this sense, the research setting supports a broader argument:

> XAI is not a narrow technical subproblem, but an increasingly important capability across multiple business decision-making contexts.

## Benchmark / Evaluation Logic

Because this is an editorial / positioning paper, it does not provide algorithmic benchmarks such as AUC, accuracy, or leaderboard tables. It is therefore more useful to interpret the paper through its **evaluation logic** rather than a benchmark setting.

The core evaluative questions implied by the paper are:

1. Does XAI help users better understand AI outputs?
2. Does XAI improve the quality of business decision-making?
3. Does XAI increase trust, transparency, and accountability?
4. Is the value of XAI visible in concrete applications rather than only in abstract technical terms?

The most important contribution of this paper is that it implicitly reframes how XAI should be evaluated:

it is not enough to ask whether explanations exist; one must ask whether they improve the decision-support process.

## Conceptual Summary

Read the paper as a decision-support chain:
- `Input`: different explainable-AI methods provide different explanation properties.
- `Core mechanism`: those properties shape human decision processes rather than acting directly on outcomes.
- `Key message`: the review is about how XAI changes decision making, not only model transparency.
## Review Logic

Because this is not an algorithmic benchmark paper, it does not evaluate model performance through formulas such as AUC or accuracy. Its logic is instead conceptual and field-oriented. The central questions are:

1. How should XAI be understood within business decision-making contexts?
2. What kinds of value does explainability create for decision support?
3. Which broad dimensions help organize research in this space?
4. What future opportunities does this framing open for IS and business scholars?

This review logic matters because it shifts attention from a purely model-centric view of explainability toward a decision-centric one.

## Literature Value and Boundaries

The main value of this paper is as a **research-positioning and evaluation-framing source** for work at the intersection of XAI, IS, and decision support. It is especially useful when a literature review needs language for connecting technical explainability to managerial usefulness.

At the same time, its limits are clear. It does not provide a technical mechanism for semantic feedback generation, prompt optimization, or factor-level faithfulness control. Its contribution is primarily conceptual and editorial rather than methodological.
